LLM Hallucinations and Factual Accuracy

Hallucination in large language models refers to the generation of text that is fluent, confident-sounding, and syntactically correct, but factually incorrect, fabricated, or unsupported by the provided context or world knowledge. It is one of the most critical reliability challenges in deploying AI systems.

Types of Hallucinations:

1. Factual Hallucinations (Closed-Domain):
The model contradicts facts in its provided context or training data. Example: citing a real paper with a wrong author, date, or conclusion.

2. Open-Domain Hallucinations:
The model fabricates information not present anywhere — inventing citations, URLs, people, or statistics.

3. Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Hallucinations:
- Intrinsic: The output directly contradicts the input source material.
- Extrinsic: The output contains information that cannot be verified or falsified from the source material.

Root Causes:
- Training data noise and conflicting facts.
- Distributional shift between pre-training and deployment.
- Overconfident generation: the model interpolates between related concepts.
- Insufficient context: the model fills gaps with plausible-sounding invented content.
- RLHF pressure to sound helpful, encouraging guessing over admitting uncertainty.

Mitigation Techniques:
- Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG): Ground responses in retrieved evidence.
- Faithfulness evaluation: Automatically score whether answers are grounded in context.
- Citation generation: Force the model to cite specific passages.
- Constitutional AI: Train models to self-critique and revise hallucinated outputs.
- Temperature reduction: Deterministic (temperature=0) sampling reduces creative fabrication.
- Calibration: Training models to express uncertainty ("I don't know").

Measuring Hallucinations:
Ragas Faithfulness metric: breaks down an answer into atomic claims and checks whether each is supported by the retrieved context using an LLM judge, producing a score in [0, 1].
